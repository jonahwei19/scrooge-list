What is the Scrooge Score?

The Scrooge Score measures how little of their wealth billionaires have deployed for charitable purposes. It's an inverse philanthropy ranking - higher scores indicate less giving relative to capacity.

The score ranges from 0-100:

  • 90-100: Extreme Scrooge - giving less than 1% of deployable wealth
  • 70-89: High Scrooge - giving 1-3% of deployable wealth
  • 40-69: Moderate Scrooge - giving 3-6% of deployable wealth
  • 0-39: Low Scrooge - giving more than 6% of deployable wealth

The Formula

Scrooge Score = 100 x (1 - Giving Ratio) Giving Ratio = Total Lifetime Giving / (Net Worth x Liquidity Factor) Capped at 0-100

The formula is intentionally simple: what percentage of your deployable wealth have you actually given away?

Why Liquidity Factor?

Not all wealth is equally accessible. A tech founder with 90% of their net worth in concentrated company stock faces real constraints on giving that someone with diversified investments does not. The liquidity factor adjusts for this reality.

Wealth Source Liquidity Factor Rationale
Diversified investments 0.70 Can sell most holdings without market impact
Public founder (diversified) 0.50 Has sold down significantly, more liquid
Public founder (concentrated) 0.30 Still holds large stake, sales move market
Private company 0.20 No public market, limited exit options
Real estate heavy 0.25 Illiquid, sales take time
Inherited/mixed 0.40 Typically more diversified over time

Data Sources & Confidence Levels

We use multiple sources to triangulate giving estimates. Each source has different reliability:

HIGH Confidence Sources

  • IRS Form 990-PF: Private foundation tax returns filed with the IRS, available via ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer. Shows actual grants made (not pledges).
  • SEC Form 4: Insider transactions including stock donations to charities. Public record.
  • University gift announcements: Major universities publicly report large gifts with donor approval.
  • Hospital/medical center records: Named buildings and major health philanthropy announcements.

MEDIUM Confidence Sources

  • Chronicle of Philanthropy: Tracks major announced gifts, relies on public announcements.
  • Giving Pledge letters: Self-reported intentions, not verified giving.
  • Foundation annual reports: Published by the foundations themselves, may include multi-year commitments.
  • Forbes/Bloomberg profiles: Aggregated from multiple sources, varying accuracy.
  • News coverage: Major gift announcements, subject to PR timing.

LOW Confidence Sources

  • Donor-Advised Fund estimates: DAFs are opaque; we can only observe transfers in, not grants out. Estimated at 0.3% of NW as a floor.
  • Philanthropic LLC estimates: Like CZI, LLCs aren't required to report. We use available data from annual reports when published.
  • Anonymous giving estimates: Some billionaires give anonymously. We don't guess at this.

What We Count

We count actual charitable disbursements, not pledges or asset transfers:

Counted Not Counted
Foundation grants to external charities Foundation assets (until granted out)
Direct gifts to nonprofits Pledges (until disbursed)
Stock donations (at time of transfer) Political donations
University endowment gifts For-profit impact investments
DAF grants to charities (when observable) DAF contributions (warehoused)
The Foundation Asset Problem

Many billionaires have transferred huge sums to private foundations that pay out at the legal minimum (5% of assets). We count foundation grants, not assets. A billionaire with $10B in a foundation making $500M/year in grants gets credit for $500M/year - not the $10B sitting in the foundation.

What We Don't Count

  • Political donations: Different purpose, not charitable.
  • Pledges: Only count when money actually moves.
  • Foundation assets: Only count grants out, not assets in.
  • Philanthropic LLCs: Counted only when they make actual grants.
  • Self-dealing: Grants to organizations the billionaire controls for personal benefit.

Deduplication

A major challenge is avoiding double-counting. Money can flow through multiple channels:

  1. Billionaire gives stock to their private foundation
  2. Foundation transfers to their DAF
  3. DAF grants to operating charity

We try to count at the final destination (the operating charity) when possible. When tracing is impossible, we use the most reliable data point available.

Worked Examples

Bill Gates

Net Worth: $129B | Liquidity: 0.30 (concentrated founder) | Deployable: $38.7B

Lifetime Giving: $59B personal + $78B foundation grants = ~$137B

Giving Ratio: $137B / $38.7B = 3.54 (exceeds 100%)

Scrooge Score: 0 (capped at 0)

Gates has given more than his theoretical "deployable" wealth because he's been giving for 30+ years while his wealth has grown.

Larry Ellison

Net Worth: $245B | Liquidity: 0.35 | Deployable: $85.75B

Lifetime Giving: ~$1.5B

Giving Ratio: $1.5B / $85.75B = 0.0175 (1.75%)

Scrooge Score: 100 x (1 - 0.0175) = 98.25

Despite signing the Giving Pledge and recently pledging 95% of his fortune, actual disbursements remain low.

Michael Bloomberg

Net Worth: $106B | Liquidity: 0.50 (diversified) | Deployable: $53B

Lifetime Giving: $21.1B

Giving Ratio: $21.1B / $53B = 0.398 (39.8%)

Scrooge Score: 100 x (1 - 0.398) = 60.2

America's top individual donor in 2023 and 2024, with $3.7B given in 2024 alone.

Limitations & Caveats

This is an estimate, not a verdict

Philanthropy is notoriously opaque. We do our best with available data, but some billionaires give anonymously, through structures we can't see, or in ways that aren't captured by public records. A high Scrooge Score might mean someone doesn't give much - or it might mean their giving is invisible to us.

Known Blind Spots

  • Anonymous giving: Some billionaires deliberately avoid publicity.
  • International giving: Non-US charities are harder to track.
  • Private foundations abroad: No 990-PF equivalent in most countries.
  • Family attribution: Hard to separate individual vs. family giving.
  • Timing: Data lags by 1-2 years for tax filings.

What This Doesn't Measure

  • Effectiveness: We count dollars, not impact.
  • Intent: Some giving is strategic, some is generous.
  • Taxes: We don't adjust for tax benefits received.
  • Future pledges: Promises don't count until fulfilled.

Updates & Corrections

This dataset is updated periodically. If you have corrections or additional verified data:

  • Open an issue on the GitHub repository
  • Include source documentation (990s, news articles, official announcements)

We prioritize corrections that include primary source documentation.
