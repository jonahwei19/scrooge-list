A Note on Method · Vol. II · April MMXXVI

Methodology

How the Scrooge List is built, what it can see, what it cannot, and why it is tiered rather than ranked.

What This List Measures

The Scrooge List estimates observable out-the-door giving to tax-exempt charitable organizations, as a share of capacity. That is the narrowest honest measure of billionaire philanthropy, and it is the only one that survives the available data. This list does not claim to measure total generosity. It cannot, because much of billionaire giving passes through vehicles that do not publicly disclose.

Every dollar we cite is linked to a source — a 990-PF filing, a charity-side announcement, a regulatory filing, a reputable press report. We do not estimate where we cannot see. Where giving is invisible, we say so.

The language we use

We write "we identified $X in documented giving," never "they have given only $X." A low observable figure means our sources could not verify more — not that more does not exist.

Tiers, Not Rankings

Ranking the Forbes 3,000 in ordinal order — who is #47 stingiest vs. #48 — would require a precision our data does not support. Observable giving is a lower-bound estimate; net worth carries a 10–25% spread across credible sources; and the residue of unobservable giving is structurally unknowable. We therefore classify:

TierWho BelongsPublished As
A — Verified LowLow observable giving and no material opaque vehicle (DAF, LLC, offshore trust) that could plausibly close the gap.Ordinal rank — by absolute dollar shortfall vs. the capacity benchmark.
B — Probably LowLow observable giving relative to capacity, but at least one opaque vehicle exists, so hidden giving is possible.Sorted by dollar shortfall; no published ordinal rank.
C — OpaqueMost giving flows through vehicles (LLCs, DAFs) that do not require public disclosure; the published figure is a floor.Sorted by dollar shortfall; no published ordinal rank.
On TrackObservable giving is near or above the capacity benchmark.Shown for calibration. Not Scrooges.

This is the approach Bloomberg uses for wealth (a 1–5 star confidence rating) and that the Chronicle of Philanthropy uses for its Philanthropy 50 (it simply declines to rank anyone who won't disclose). We adopt the same discipline, inverted: where we can't see, we don't rank.

The Capacity Benchmark

Expected Giving = 5% × Liquid Wealth × Years as Billionaire • Liquid wealth: net worth × liquidity factor (0.15 – 0.85 depending on asset mix)
• Tenure is not capped. A 40-year billionaire carries eight times the expectation of a 5-year one.
• The headline ranking is absolute dollar shortfall = Expected − Observable, not the ratio.

The 5%-per-year coefficient is deliberately conservative. It matches the IRS private-foundation legal minimum payout rate (5% annual), sits below the lifetime-pledge benchmark (50% minimum, per the Giving Pledge, which implies ≈2.5%/yr across a 20-year horizon), and approximates the aggregate sustainable giving rate for an endowment held forever. Deploying 5% of liquid wealth annually is the mildest version of the pledge ethic.

Why Absolute Shortfall, Not Ratio

An earlier draft of this list ranked Tier A by the ratio of observable giving to expected capacity — i.e., fraction. That gave counter-intuitive results. Ira Rennert ($3.8B net worth, 30 years as a billionaire, $13M observable giving) ranked above Elon Musk ($811B, 14 years, $500M observable) because Rennert's ratio was marginally lower.

The ratio framing flattens scale. A $500M gift from someone with $811B of wealth is not philanthropically equivalent to a $500M gift from someone with $2B — and a $170B-undisbursed-capacity gap is not equivalent to a $2B-undisbursed gap. The absolute dollar shortfall is what matters to the underlying question: how much charitable capacity is being left on the table?

Worked example. Under this methodology:
  • Larry Page — $269B net worth, ~33% liquid, 20 years → expected ≈ $89B; observable $1.8B → shortfall ~$87B. Ranks #1.
  • Elon Musk — $811B net worth, ~21% liquid, 14 years → expected ≈ $85B; observable $500M → shortfall ~$85B. Ranks #2.
  • Thomas Peterffy — $83B net worth, mostly liquid, ~40 years → expected ≈ $29B; observable $49M → shortfall ~$29B. Ranks #3.
  • Ira Rennert — $3.8B, low liquidity, 30 years → expected ≈ $0.9B; observable $13M → shortfall ~$0.8B. Last in Tier A.
The ratio figures remain visible on each profile as a calibration tool, but the published ranking is by dollar shortfall.

What We Cannot See — The Honest Error Bar

Observable ≠ Total

Research across IPS, Boston College's Forum on Philanthropy, and the National Philanthropic Trust suggests that observable 990-based and announcement-based giving captures roughly 40–70% of true lifetime giving for a typical US billionaire cohort. The residual is real and large. A high Scrooge tier does not assert the subject is ungenerous — it asserts we cannot find evidence of generosity at scale.

Channels with structural opacity:

  • Donor-Advised Funds — $326B held at US sponsors (NPT 2025); 25.3% annual payout; 27% of all individual giving now flows through DAFs. No donor-level disclosure.
  • Philanthropic LLCs — CZI (Zuckerberg), Emerson Collective (Powell Jobs), Ballmer Group, Bezos Earth Fund parent (Fellowship Ventures), and Good Ventures (Moskovitz) are LLCs with no 990 filing requirement. Some (Moskovitz) publish extensively; others (CZI) publish selectively. Transparency is a choice, not a requirement.
  • Dynasty Trusts — $360B+ in South Dakota alone (ICIJ Pandora Papers); no public registry; capable of running for centuries.
  • 501(c)(4) Advocacy — Politically active charities; not philanthropy under standard methodology. Koch's Believe in People received $4.3B in 2022 alone.
  • Anonymous Giving — Per the 2024 BofA/Lilly HNW study, 69% of older HNW donors report giving anonymously at least sometimes.
  • Religious Giving — Churches are exempt from 990 filings. 39% of affluent donor dollars go to religion (2025 BofA/Lilly).
  • Foreign Giving — No country discloses donor names in bulk. Non-US giving by US billionaires (and vice versa) is largely invisible.

Deduplication Rules

A single charitable event can appear in three places: a press announcement, the donor's 990-PF (if routed through their foundation), and the recipient's 990 (if filed). Counting all three would triple-count. We apply these rules:

RuleWhat It Does
Pledge ≠ PaymentA 2010 $10B pledge and a 2024 $500M payment are different events. Pledges are tracked but do not count as observable until disbursed.
Into ≠ Out ofA billionaire transferring stock to their private foundation is a transfer_in. The foundation's subsequent grants to working charities are grant_out. Only the latter counts as observable giving. (Forbes' methodology is the reverse, which IPS has criticized.)
Announcement + FilingIf a $200M press-announced gift also appears in that year's 990-PF, it is the same event. Recorded once.
DAF-routed announced giftIf the donor publicly says "we gave $10M to X via our DAF," this counts as a direct gift, not DAF opacity.
Multi-year pledgeCounted in the year paid, not the year announced. (Chronicle uses the opposite; we document both framings.)

What Does Not Count as Charity

We exclude from observable giving:

  • For-profit "impact" ventures. The Ellison Institute of Technology (Oxford) is structured as a for-profit hybrid per reporting in the New York Times (Aug 2025); under the Forbes, Chronicle, and IPS methodologies it does not count as charity until and unless disbursed to a 501(c)(3) or foreign charitable equivalent.
  • Private investments framed as philanthropy. Ellison's December 2025 $40B commitment to the Paramount–Skydance merger was described by Fortune as "not a charitable donation in the classic sense."
  • 501(c)(4) advocacy contributions. Political advocacy, including to "social welfare" organizations that function as super-PAC adjuncts.
  • Political contributions. Federal, state, and super-PAC giving. Tracked in a separate field and flagged as POLITICAL_HEAVY when large, because NBER Working Paper 26616 estimates political giving displaces charitable giving at roughly $0.33 per $1.
  • Sports team ownership, political campaigns, and commercial real estate. Even when philanthropically framed.

Right of Reply

Every Tier A subject is contacted before publication.

We send a plain-text email to the subject's known press or foundation contact, stating the specific numbers and tier we intend to publish, and offering a one-week response window. Any response — verbatim — is published on the subject's profile. "No response" is published in the same place.

This is standard practice at Forbes, Bloomberg, and the Chronicle of Philanthropy. It is also the single most effective defense against the actual malice standard applied in US defamation law to public figures.

Corrections

We maintain a public changelog. If you can document giving we have missed — with a source we can link — send it and we will update the record. Preferred: 990-PF line-items, recipient-side press releases, SEC filings, pledge letters. We do not update on bare assertion.

Known Limitations

  • US-centric. This first release covers US-resident billionaires where 990-PF and SEC Form 4 data anchor verification. Global cohort (Hurun, ICIJ, UK/CA/AU registries) is deferred to the next release.
  • Recency. 990-PF filings lag 6–18 months. Recent large gifts (2025–2026) may be announced but not yet in foundation filings.
  • Net worth denominator. Forbes and Bloomberg disagree by 10–25% for donors with concentrated private holdings. We publish a range, not a point estimate.
  • Name matching. Small foundations may be missed; namesakes (two Knight Foundations exist, for example — one Phil Knight, one Miami journalism) are flagged manually.
  • Impact is not measured. A $100M gift to a high-impact charity and a $100M gift to a named building appear identically in our data. This is a list of inputs, not outcomes.
  • Historical cutoff. Gifts before the subject's billionaire era are generally not tracked.

What We Publish Per Subject

Every Tier A and Tier B profile displays:

  1. Best-estimate net worth, range across credible sources, and as-of date.
  2. Observable giving with every dollar linked to its source URL.
  3. Detected vehicles (active foundations, closed foundations, LLCs, offshore entities, trusts-of-interest).
  4. Pledge status (Giving Pledge signed? letter text? fulfillment per IPS 2025?).
  5. Political giving floor + source.
  6. Red flags with citations.
  7. Confidence interval: [observable, observable + hidden upper bound].
  8. Right-of-reply box — subject's response or "no response to date X."
  9. Full source log with retrieval dates.
  10. Verification TODOs — what we have not yet checked.
